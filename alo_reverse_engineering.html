Gap Analysis — Where Model and Reality Diverge
Four distinct findings from the reverse-engineering exercise

Strong Validations

Top markets correctly predicted

Every MSA scoring above 5.5 in the recalibrated model has confirmed Alo® presence, validating the variable set and directional logic.

  • SF-Oakland #1 (6.33): 2 stores — lifestyle + wealth combo confirmed
  • Seattle #2 (6.10): Zero stores — but the model is right; Alo® simply hasn't arrived yet
  • Austin #4 (6.02): 1 store at The Domain — strong pipeline signal
  • Miami #5 (5.83): 5 stores — lifestyle + tourism + migration all confirmed
  • Denver #11 (5.35): 3 stores — outdoor lifestyle + WF density nailed it
  • Boston #6 (5.71): 2 stores — education wealth concentration confirmed

Highest-Priority True Gaps

High score, very few Alo® stores

Markets with proven affluent customer demand (validated by Lululemon density) where Alo® has not yet captured its fair share.

  • Seattle (6.10, 0 stores, 10 Lulu): The single most compelling gap. Tech wealth, PNW outdoor culture, zero Alo® presence. Westfield Bellevue and University Village are obvious targets.
  • Austin (6.02, 1 store, 8 Lulu): The Domain is open. South Congress, Hill Country Galleria, and Domain NORTHSIDE are natural next steps.
  • San Diego (5.69, 1 store, 8 Lulu): Fashion Valley is open. UTC mall and Del Mar Highlands logical additions.
  • Dallas (5.31, 1 store, 14 Lulu): Severely under-indexed. NorthPark is open. Highland Park Village, Legacy West and Willow Bend are prime targets.
  • Raleigh-Durham (4.84, 1 store, 6 Lulu): North Hills open. Triangle Town Center or Cary Towne next.

Model Misses — Calibration Signals

Low score but multiple Alo® stores

Two markets exposed structural gaps in the original model, already addressed in the recalibrated weights:

  • Chicago (v1 rank 22, 3 actual stores): We underweighted urban prestige and tourism. The Mag Mile flagship is a brand-statement investment — 55M annual visitors justify it even with population decline. Tourism weight increased from 10% → 14%.
  • Philadelphia (v1 rank 26, 3 actual stores): King of Prussia is a top-5 grossing US mall by sales/sqft. Our model counted Class A malls by quantity, not quality — a Tier 2 variable (individual mall sales ranking) would capture this. Chicago rises to rank 19 and Philadelphia to rank 27 in v2 — still conservative but much improved.
  • Coverage strategy markets: Columbus, Louisville, Indianapolis, Oklahoma City — Alo® is planting geographic anchor flags in regional market leaders regardless of luxury profile. Our model doesn't capture this coverage mandate and intentionally does not try to — it focuses on premium expansion opportunity.

New Markets Added in v2

10 MSAs outside original scope

These markets had confirmed Alo® stores but were absent from the original 29-MSA model. All scored and ranked in v2:

  • Las Vegas (rank 25, 3 stores): Tourism-dominated model — Wynn, Fashion Show, Summerlin. Hotel density metric properly capped to prevent distortion.
  • Sacramento (rank 30, 1 store): Westfield Galleria Roseville — Bay Area spillover customer base
  • Jacksonville (rank 31, 1 store): St. Johns Town Center — FL growth market, lower lifestyle score

  • Oklahoma City, Birmingham: Coverage plays; both score below 2.0
What Alo®'s Location Strategy Reveals
Six strategic patterns extracted from the actual 76+ store portfolio

Pattern 1: Cluster, Then Expand

Density before diversification

Alo® opened 9 stores in LA, 12 in NY, and 5 in Miami before entering most other markets. The cluster-first approach builds brand density, operational efficiency, and community momentum. Markets with 0–1 stores (Seattle, Austin, Dallas) are likely next for cluster-building.

Pattern 2: Mall Quality > Mall Count

Elite venues over broad presence

King of Prussia, Bal Harbour, Americana Manhasset, Wynn Las Vegas — Alo® consistently picks the single best venue in a market rather than multiple average ones. This is a clear Tier 2 signal: individual mall prestige score should outweigh total metro mall count.

Pattern 3: Street + Mall Dual Play

Flagships for brand, malls for volume

In mature markets, Alo® opens both: street flagships (SoHo, Mag Mile, Georgetown, Beverly Hills Canon Dr.) for brand elevation, and mall locations (Hudson Yards, Century City, Oakbrook) for foot traffic and volume. Both channels serve different objectives.

Pattern 4: Lifestyle Centers Over-Index

24% of portfolio in open-air lifestyle

The Domain, Kierland Commons, Cherry Creek, 3rd Street Promenade — Alo® disproportionately favors open-air lifestyle centers where the outdoor/wellness brand aesthetic resonates naturally. These also tend to have high WF density and yoga studio co-tenancy, directly validating our lifestyle variable selection.

Pattern 5: The Lululemon Co-location Signal

Where Lulu is, Alo® follows

In nearly every market analyzed, Alo® located within 0.5–2 miles of a Lululemon store, often in the same mall. This validates Lululemon store count as the single strongest leading indicator: it confirms the customer, justifies the rent, and marks the territory Alo® is explicitly competing for. Dallas with 14 Lulu vs. 1 Alo® is the clearest example of uncaptured validated demand.

Pattern 6: Geographic Coverage Mandate

Brand grid beyond pure ROI

Louisville, Oklahoma City, Birmingham, Columbus — these don't fit the luxury consumer model. What they share: the top Class A mall in their regional market, growing population, minimal competition. Alo® is systematically planting flags in regional market leaders to build a national presence grid. This coverage strategy is tracked separately from premium expansion opportunity in our model.
